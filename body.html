Improvements in the Economic Domain

Cryptocurrencies

One topic that springs to mind when we talk about technology in the economic domain is the use of cryptocurrencies. A cryptocurrency is a digital or virtual currency that is meant to be a medium of exchange. Key characteristics of cryptocurrencies include the existence of limits on the of number units that can exist, they operate independently to a bank and makes it very easy to verify the transfer of funds. Most modern day transactions are quite fluid and problem free, however problems can still occur when we make an online transaction such as a technical issue with our bank or a hacker making his way into your accounts.


Cryptocurrency was created to try and get around all these rare but existential problems one might face when making a digital transaction in the modern world. If two people were to make a transaction using a cryptocurrency there would be short processing period where the app used would make sure it is the correct individual authoring the payment (face ID with Apple pay). Once that processing period is completed the transaction is completed within minutes. As of 2018, there was more than 1600 cryptocurrencies available and almost every single day a new cryptocurrency is created. If Cryptocurrencies are used quite extensively it is mainly due to the fact that they have little to no transaction fees, users get 24/7 access to money, they are free for anyone to use and they make international payments much faster and easier.



The Growth of the Computing Industry

Let us take the example of Apple, Apple Inc. was founded on the 1st of 1976 by Steve Jobs and Steve Wozniak. This company was created to try and innovate the computer industry by creating a computer that people could have in their homes and offices. We must keep in mind that at the time where Apple was founded, computers were large machines that would take up the majority of any room in which they were in.

Apple quickly became very much successful in a few years with its commercialisation of the Apple II, the very first miniaturised computer Apple created which contained a monitor, keyboard and CPU. With this new innovation sales for Apple jumped from $7.8 million in 1978 to $117 million in 1980. From its expansion to modern times, Apple has never stop pushing the boundaries of computing as they make increasingly sophisticated technological products. A very good example of this is when in 2007 Apple commercialised the iPhone, a new product that is small enough for people to carry around, offering many essential services such as access to internet, GPS, photos, etc. Before the iPhone, most smartphones did not offer as many services as the iPhone and were not very user friendly. The commercialisation of the iPhone led to Apple taking the lead in the technological industry and therefore surpassing Nokia, Blackberry and Microsoft. Apple has continued to innovate the iPhone and have been continuing to improve this product throughout the years.
